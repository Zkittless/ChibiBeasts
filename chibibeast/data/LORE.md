# 🐾 The ChibiBeasts Lore Bible
### *The Sundering of the Loom*

---

## I. The Creation Myth

Before there were beasts, before there were trainers, before there was even a *world* to stand on — there was only **the Loom**.

The Loom was not a place. It was the act of weaving itself: an endless, formless process spinning raw possibility into shape. It had no name, because there was no one to name it, and no mouth to speak one.

Then, in a single instant that some call the **First Stitch**, the Loom wove four threads tighter than any others — and they woke up.

These four became the **Architects**: not gods exactly, not quite alive in the way a fairy or a goblin is alive, but *aware*. Each Architect grasped one piece of what the Loom was doing and gave it a shape a small creature could hold.

- **Prism**, the Architect of Form, who believed reality needed structure — edges, geometry, order. From a shard of its own crystalline heart, it shaped **Prismite**, a small porcelain kitten with diamond eyes, and sent it out to see whether *order* could be gentle.
- **Twine**, the Architect of Time, who believed reality needed memory — a thread connecting what-was to what-will-be. It pulled loose a strand of itself, crimson and ticking, and shaped it into a hamster with a tiny clock-gear shell: **Twine**, sent to learn whether *time* could be kind.
- **Aspect**, the Architect of Change, who believed reality needed to be soft enough to become anything. It let a piece of its own shifting, galaxy-colored self bubble free into **Gloop**, a slime full of swirling starlight, sent to discover whether *change* could be safe.
- **Pillar**, the Architect of Foundation, who believed reality needed something that would not break. It cracked off a fragment of bark and ancient moss and stone and shaped **Barkley**, a small dragon hatchling wearing its own eggshell like a helmet, sent to prove that *steadiness* could still be playful.

Each Architect sent its small creation down into the unfinished world — alone, unfinished too, but capable of choosing a companion. That is the oldest story ChibiBeasts tells about itself: **the world began with four lonely ideas, and they did not want to be lonely anymore.**

This is why, to this day, every trainer's journey begins with a choice between **Prismite**, **Twine**, **Gloop**, or **Barkley**. You are not picking a pet. You are continuing a four-sided conversation that started before the world had a floor to stand on.

## II. The Five Divine Collections

The Architects didn't stop at four companions. As the Loom kept weaving, larger ideas crystallized into Divine beings — vast, beautiful, and a little bit beyond understanding. They organized themselves (or were organized, nobody's quite sure) into five Collections, each one a different answer to the question *"what does it mean for something to be eternal?"*

| Collection | Domain | Members |
|---|---|---|
| **Cosmic Creators** | The raw stuff reality is made of | Singularity, Astraea, Chronos, Aetherius |
| **Architects of Reality** | Beginnings, endings, and the space between | Genesis, Terminus, Paradox |
| **Celestial Loom** | Fate, consequence, and the threads connecting all things | Horizon, Karma, Zodiac |
| **Primordial Aspects** | Forces too big for a single shape — gravity, decay, combustion | Abyss, Nebula, Supernova |
| **Mythological Pillars** | The things that hold the world up and refuse to fall | Nirvana, Asgard, Atlas |

Trainers don't typically *meet* Divines so much as **earn the right to be noticed by one** — through raids, through deep exploration of the Celestial Loom biome, or (the long way) through the loyalty of an ordinary beast raised with enough care that something Divine takes interest.

The five Collections are not rivals. They're closer to five departments of the same impossible organization, each one quietly making sure the world keeps being a world. Most of the time, anyway.

## III. The Sundering — What Actually Happened

Here's the part that matters for raids.

A long time after the Architects sent their four companions out, after Common and Uncommon and Rare beasts had filled in every forest and trench and cave, something the Loom wove went *wrong*.

Not evil exactly — the Loom doesn't really do evil. But it tried to weave something too big, too fast, and the thread **snapped**.

What came loose is called an **Altered Divine**: a being that was *supposed* to become something like Genesis or Atlas, but the weaving fractured halfway through. The result is a Divine-scale creature without a finished shape — unstable, in pain in a way that doesn't translate to words, and instinctively trying to finish becoming itself by **consuming whatever's nearby**. Mana, gold, beasts, sometimes entire biomes' worth of stability.

This is why raid bosses appear: an Altered Divine has torn into a region, and it won't stop tearing until either (a) it successfully finishes weaving itself into a stable shape — which is catastrophic for everyone nearby — or (b) a large enough group of trainers fights it down long enough for the Loom to "recapture" the loose thread and properly finish the weave. A successful raid doesn't kill the Altered Divine. **It finally finishes being born — correctly, this time** — which is why defeating one occasionally lets a trainer walk away with a small, purified piece of what it was always supposed to become.

The first recorded Sundering produced the **Anomalous Nebula**, also called the Supernova Singularity — a cloud dragon that collapsed in on itself mid-weave and now phases between being a black hole and being a thundercloud, unable to settle on which. It was *supposed* to be Supernova, of the Primordial Aspects. It still might be, if enough trainers ever finish the job.

## IV. Why You're Doing Any of This

Putting it plainly, for the player: **you are not an exterminator, a soldier, or a chosen one.** You're a trainer in a world that is, very literally, still being woven — and every beast you catch, raise, battle with, and bond with is part of how that weaving gets *gentler* instead of rougher.

- Catching and hatching beasts mirrors what the Architects did at the very beginning: giving a small, unfinished idea a companion so it isn't alone.
- Battling isn't conquest — beasts in ChibiBeasts don't fight to the death, they fight to test and grow, the same way Prismite, Twine, Gloop, and Barkley were sent out to "see whether their Architect's idea could survive contact with the world."
- Raiding an Altered Divine is, quite directly, **helping something finish being born** — the highest-stakes, highest-stress form of the same caretaking instinct the whole game is built on.
- The Bestiary exists because the Loom *itself* seems to want to be witnessed — every first catch in a server is treated, narratively, as a tiny stitch finishing correctly.

This is also why the tone stays warm even at endgame: there's no final boss to slay and no apocalypse to prevent. The "stakes" are always really just *"help this unfinished thing settle into a shape it can be happy with."* That's true of a wild Slime in the Whispering Woods and it's true of a server-shaking Altered Divine raid boss. Same instinct, different scale.

## V. Naming & Tone Guide (for future content)

To keep new beasts, items, and systems feeling like they belong in this world:

- **Beasts** are never "monsters" in dialogue/flavor text — they're referred to the way you'd refer to a animal companion: by what they *do*, not what they *are for*. ("A delicate woodland sprite that flutters through enchanted forests," not "a low-tier nature-type unit.")
- **Divine beasts' descriptions always gesture at a cosmic-scale concept made small and almost-graspable** — they're vast ideas, chibi-sized. Keep the contrast between "incomprehensible cosmic force" and "fits in your party" — that gap *is* the charm of the whole game.
- **Items and materials use a kitchen-table-magic register**: things smell like strawberries, taste like cinnamon, look like candy — even Legendary/Divine-tier items stay tactile and a little silly, never grimdark. A "Tear of the Creator" should still sound like something you could (carefully) put in your pocket.
- **No real death.** Beasts faint, eggs fail to hatch and need to be tried again, Altered Divines are "finished," never "killed." This isn't a content restriction so much as a tone commitment — it's baked into why raids work the way they do above.
- **The Loom is never a villain**, and there's deliberately no single antagonist. Instability (Altered Divines) is closer to weather than to evil — something to respond to with care and teamwork, not something to hate.

## VI. Hooks for Future Systems

This lore was written specifically to make the next systems easy to design honestly, rather than as decoration bolted onto mechanics that already existed. Some direct threads to pull on:

- **Starters (Prismite/Twine/Gloop/Barkley):** currently `/start` hands out a random Common instead of these four. Fixing that is now also a *lore* fix, not just a code fix — these four are supposed to be the very first beasts every trainer can choose.
- **Beast Dispositions/Natures:** can be framed as "how loudly your beast's Architect's original idea still echoes in them" — a Whimsical disposition leans Twine/Aspect-flavored, an Ancient disposition leans Pillar-flavored, etc. Gives the random stat-roll system actual meaning instead of being pure mechanics.
- **Egg Incubation:** eggs can be framed as "still-weaving" beasts — narratively consistent with calling a hatch "finishing" rather than "spawning."
- **Equipment/Crafting materials:** every material already sourced from a named beast (Dragon Scale, Phoenix Ash, etc.) is implicitly "a piece of that beast's idea, repurposed with consent" — worth a one-line crafting-flavor convention so gear never feels like trophy-hunting.
- **Guild Sanctuaries:** can be framed as small, deliberately-built echoes of what the Architects did at the start — a guild "weaving" its own little stable space together.
- **Altered Divine Raids:** the mechanical and narrative purpose are now the same thing (described in Section III) — raid rewards, raid framing text, and the eventual "Corrupted/Ancient" raid tiers mentioned in your design notes can all hang off "the Loom snapping" as the unifying cause.
